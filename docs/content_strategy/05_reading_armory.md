## Tier 1: The Stoic Core

### Marcus Aurelius — *Meditations* (~170 AD)
**Hook:** *The most powerful man in the world wrote this while his soldiers were dying of plague and his general was staging a coup. He wrote it for himself. You're reading his private notes.*
**Why:** The original field manual for governing yourself under impossible pressure. Written by the most powerful man on earth while his empire was cracking. He is the prototype of the Architect. His line "Do not act as if you had ten thousand years to throw away" and the 5th Avenue photograph (1900→1913) are saying the same thing across 1,800 years: *you do not have time.* The ancient and the modern collapse into a single urgency.
**Key concepts:** (1) Governance of the guiding principle under chaos. (2) The present moment is the only life you have. (3) All rational beings are kin. (4) Death is a natural action, not a punishment. (5) Opinion is the source of suffering, not events.
**Where:** Ch 1 (The Photograph — temporal collapse), Ch 6 (The Dart — Book II.1), Ch 18 (The Ordo — Book VI.54), Ch 21 (Daily Practice — Book V.1), Ch 22 (The Passage — Book XII.36), and threaded throughout.
**Legal:** Public domain (1887 Collier/Zimmern translation). Full passages may be embedded and narrated.
**Source file:** `reference/meditations.txt`

### Epictetus — *Enchiridion* (~135 AD)
**Hook:** *He was born a slave. His master broke his leg. He became the most influential philosopher in Rome. His handbook is 20 pages long. It will rewire how you process suffering.*
**Why:** The handbook of a man who was born a slave and became the most influential Stoic teacher in Rome. If you want proof that external circumstances cannot define you, this is it.
**Key concepts:** (1) The dichotomy of control — only your judgments are yours. (2) Desire what you have, not what you lack. (3) Practice discomfort deliberately. (4) You are an actor in a play you did not write.
**Where:** Ch 6 (The Dart), Ch 8 (The Internal Cockpit).
**Legal:** Public domain. Full passages may be embedded.
**Source file:** `reference/classics/Epictetus - Enchiridion.txt`

### Seneca — *Morals of a Happy Life, Benefits, Anger and Clemency* (~65 AD)
**Hook:** *The richest man in Rome was a Stoic. He managed more money than most hedge funds and wrote about anger management with the precision of a neuroscientist. He proves Stoicism is not poverty cosplay.*
**Why:** Seneca was the richest man in Rome and a Stoic. He wrote about managing wealth, anger, and fortune with operational specificity. He proves that Stoicism is not poverty cosplay — it works at every economic level.
**Key concepts:** (1) A happy life is one in accordance with nature. (2) Anger is temporary madness — build systems to prevent it. (3) Benefits given freely need no return. (4) Clemency is strategic strength, not weakness.
**Where:** Ch 9 (The Question Engine), Ch 13 (Carnegie Rebuilt).
**Legal:** Public domain. Full passages may be embedded.
**Source file:** `reference/classics/Seneca - Morals of a Happy Life, Benefits, Anger and Clemency.txt`

## Tier 2: The Socratic Method

### Plato — *Apology* (~399 BC)
**Hook:** *The state of Athens executed a man for asking questions. His last speech is the most dangerous document in Western civilization.*
**Why:** Socrates's defense at trial. He was sentenced to death for asking questions. This is the foundational text for the "asking questions is the meta-skill" thesis.
**Key concepts:** (1) The unexamined life is not worth living. (2) Knowing that you don't know is the beginning of wisdom. (3) Questions are more dangerous than answers. (4) The state killed a man because his questions threatened the institution.
**Where:** Ch 4 (The Socratic Divide), Ch 9 (The Question Engine).
**Legal:** Public domain. Full passages may be embedded.
**Source file:** `reference/classics/Plato - Apology.txt`

### Plato — *Republic* (~380 BC)
**Hook:** *2,400 years ago, Plato described a cave where people watch shadows and think they're seeing reality. He described your Instagram feed.*
**Why:** The philosopher-king thesis. The allegory of the Cave. Ordo literally builds the thing Plato described: a system where the person who sees reality (the sovereign solopreneur) governs their own domain.
**Key concepts:** (1) The Cave allegory — most people see shadows, not reality. (2) The philosopher-king must govern reluctantly. (3) Justice is each part performing its proper function. (4) Education is not filling a vessel but turning the soul.
**Where:** Ch 4 (The Socratic Divide), Ch 9 (The Question Engine — Cave allegory).
**Legal:** Public domain. Full passages may be embedded.
**Source file:** `reference/classics/Plato - Republic.txt`

## Tier 4: The Modern Framework

### Nassim Taleb — *Antifragile* (2012)
**Hook:** *Taleb made $40 million betting on the 2008 crash. This book explains the system he used — and why your $10/month SQLite stack follows the same principles.*
**Why:** The entire Studio Ordo architecture is antifragile by design. The single-node SQLite stack doesn't just survive stress — it gets stronger. Taleb's framework explains why the solopreneur model outperforms the corporate model under volatility.
**Key concepts:** (1) Antifragile systems gain from disorder. (2) The barbell strategy: combine extreme safety with extreme risk, avoid the middle. (3) Skin in the game: the AI has none, you do. (4) Via negativa: what you remove matters more than what you add. (5) The Lindy Effect: what has survived will continue to survive.
**Where:** Ch 15 (The $10 Sovereignty Stack).
**Legal:** Copyrighted. Operational briefing only — no substantial quotation. Direct reader to purchase.

### Malcolm Gladwell — *Blink* (2005)
**Hook:** *A firefighter walks into a burning building, feels something wrong, and orders everyone out. The floor collapses ten seconds later. He can't explain how he knew. Gladwell can.*
**Why:** The scientific basis for the human advantage. Thin-slicing — the ability to make accurate judgments in 2 seconds — requires empathy, context, and embodied experience. This is the "you cannot outrun the Bugatti, but the Bugatti cannot read a room" argument with research behind it.
**Key concepts:** (1) Thin-slicing: rapid cognition is often superior to deliberation. (2) The adaptive unconscious processes vast amounts of data invisibly. (3) Expertise enables better snap judgments, not worse ones. (4) Context and environment contaminate or enable good judgment.
**Where:** Ch 9 (The Question Engine — human rapid cognition), Ch 2 (The Bugatti — what humans do that machines cannot).
**Legal:** Copyrighted. Operational briefing only.

## Tier 5: Supporting Framework

### Ralph Waldo Emerson — *Essays* (1841)
**Why:** "Self-Reliance" is the sovereignty thesis in 19th-century American English. Emerson wrote the philosophical foundation for every person who refused to be defined by their institution.
**Key concepts:** (1) Trust thyself. (2) Society is a conspiracy against the manhood of every one of its members. (3) A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds. (4) Imitation is suicide.
**Where:** Ch 4 (The Socratic Divide), Ch 8 (The Internal Cockpit).
**Legal:** Public domain. Full passages may be embedded.
**Source file:** `reference/classics/Emerson - Essays.txt`

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics* (~340 BC)
**Why:** Virtue is a practice, not a theory. Aristotle proved that you become good by doing good things — formation is habits, not beliefs. This is the philosophical foundation of Part V (The Formation).
**Key concepts:** (1) Virtue is a mean between extremes. (2) You become what you repeatedly do. (3) Eudaimonia (flourishing) is the highest good. (4) Practical wisdom (phronesis) is the master virtue.
**Where:** Ch 18 (The Ordo), Ch 20 (The Passage).
**Legal:** Public domain. Full passages may be embedded.
**Source file:** `reference/classics/Aristotle - Nicomachean Ethics.txt`

### Al Ries & Laura Ries — *22 Immutable Laws of Branding* (1998)
