# Chapter 5 — Editorial Memo

## Factual Corrections (Rule 1)

| Claim | Before | After | Why |
|-------|--------|-------|-----|
| Student debt | "an average of $80,000 for a private four-year institution" | "averages over $30,000 at graduation — and significantly more at many private institutions" | $80,000 is roughly double the actual average for private nonprofit bachelor's degrees ($33k–$40k per Education Data Initiative). The revised phrasing cites the more accurate baseline while acknowledging the higher end of the distribution. Attribution added. |
| Adjunct percentage | "sixty percent of courses are taught by professionals who cannot afford health insurance" | "roughly half of all instructional positions are filled by contingent faculty, many of whom lack access to health insurance" | "Sixty percent" could not be verified — adjuncts account for approximately 40% of the higher education workforce per CUPA-HR 2024-25 data. "Roughly half" is defensible given varying definitions of contingent vs. full-time. "Cannot afford" → "lack access to" — the issue is institutional eligibility, not personal affordability. |
| Titanic tonnage | "46,000 tons" | Preserved | GRT was 46,328 — rounding to 46,000 is reasonable and verified. |
| All Titanic facts | Fleet testimony, 20 lifeboats / 1,178 capacity, orchestra details, Wallace Hartley, Walter Lord 1955 | Preserved | All verified against primary inquiry transcripts and standard secondary sources. |

## Rhetorical Vanity Reductions (Rule 6)

| Before | After | Why |
|--------|-------|-----|
| "The corporation that employed you for twenty years is the *Titanic.* The career path that promised advancement in exchange for loyalty is the *Titanic.* The retirement plan that assumed a stable industry until sixty-five is the *Titanic.* The credential that cost six figures and guaranteed nothing except debt — that is the *Titanic.*" | "The career path that promised advancement in exchange for loyalty, the retirement plan that assumed a stable industry until sixty-five, the credential that cost six figures and guaranteed nothing except debt — these are the *Titanic.*" | Four-beat rhetorical anaphora compressed to a single listing sentence. The repetition of "is the *Titanic*" four times was posed — it performed the metaphor rather than deploying it. One sentence carries the same freight. |
| "The crystal chandeliers swung in widening arcs but did not go out." | Cut | Ornamental. The "lights stayed on" motif is already established. The chandelier detail was atmospheric decoration that the chapter did not need — it was reaching for cinema. |
| "care, dressed in bluntness, because bluntness is all I have left" | "care, delivered as bluntness, because the situation does not leave room for anything softer" | "Dressed in bluntness" is a metaphor-within-a-metaphor. "All I have left" dramatizes the speaker unnecessarily. The revised version grounds the bluntness in the situation rather than in the speaker's exhaustion. |
| "The lights are still on. The orchestra is still playing. And I can see the water." (ending of Grief section) | "And the institution — your institution, my institution — is still printing brochures." | The three-part motif ("lights / orchestra / water") was used three times across the chapter. Reducing to two preserves the pattern without over-reliance. The grief section now ends on "printing brochures" — more specific and less theatrical. |
| "and the champagne still chilled in the dining saloon" | Cut (appeared in two locations) | Atmospheric detail used twice, adding color but no argument. One instance was already redundant. |

## Diagrammatic Scaffolding Removed (Filter 4)

| Before | After | Why |
|--------|-------|-----|
| "Chapter 1 was fear. Chapter 4 was anger. This is grief — and grief is the one you can't skip, even though you will want to." | "I want to name what this chapter is asking you to feel, because I think writers owe readers that honesty. / This chapter is asking you to grieve." | The three-chapter emotional mapping ("fear, anger, grief") announces the book's diagrammatic scaffolding. It tells the reader that the emotional beats are planned rather than inevitable. Removing it lets the grief arrive without the reader seeing the architect's blueprint. |
