"Long enough" was slightly self-important. "Sit with them" echoes the earlier "I want you to sit with that number" — creating a quiet structural rhyme. "At once" is simpler than "simultaneously." |

## 5. Places Where the Chapter Became More Inevitable in Tone

**The thesis section.** The three observations are no longer introduced with "The first is that… The second is that… The third is that…" The ordinal framework was the chapter's most visible piece of scaffolding. Removing it lets the argument build as a continuous deepening rather than a numbered list. The reader now moves from one observation to the next without the text pausing to announce the transition. The effect: the ideas feel discovered rather than delivered.

**The "good enough" paragraph.** The aphorism was removed and replaced with a sentence that does the same work embedded in the argument: "industries do not survive the moment when 'good enough at lower cost' becomes available, because the decision to switch is not made by the people who love the old system. It is made by the people who pay for it." This says more than the aphorism did, in a voice that sounds analytical rather than epigrammatic.

**The paradigm observation.** The original "A paradigm does not announce itself" was the chapter's second most excerpt-ready line. The replacement — "No one living inside a working paradigm experiences it as a paradigm" — is less shareable and more true-sounding. The emphasis shifts from the paradigm as a personified actor to the lived experience of the people inside it, which is more consistent with the chapter's own argument about invisible infrastructure.

**The closing section.** "The crossing was survivable. It was always survivable" lost its second sentence. "Catch each other when they stumble" became "hold each other accountable when the work gets hard." "Reveal the cockpit" became "show you the cockpit." Each change is small. Together they bring the close into a register that is decisive without being arranged.
