# The Student Field Guide: Need → Signal → Style → Proof → CTA → Publish → Repeat

## What This Is

This chapter is the shortest complete version of the identity portfolio system.

Use it when you are building and need a practical anchor, not more theory. It distills the full 11-layer system (Book II, Chapter 2) into seven steps that can be executed in a single focused work session.

**It is not about inventing a brand.** It is about making it easier for the right person to understand you, trust your work, and know what to do next.

## The Seven-Step Chain

```
Need → Signal → Style → Proof → CTA → Publish → Repeat
```

If you forget everything else in this curriculum, remember that chain. Every other chapter in Books II–VI exists to give you the depth to execute each step well. This chapter gives you enough to start.

## Step 1: Need — Start with the Tension

What does your audience actually care about or worry about?

Do not start with your tools. Do not start with your credentials. Do not start with your years of experience. Start with the specific tension the right person brings into your page.

Examples of starting correctly:
- "Hiring managers at AI-forward companies don't know how to tell which candidates can actually build in production."
- "Small businesses need AI workflows but can't afford an enterprise vendor or a full team."
- "Students with strong technical skills keep losing to candidates whose portfolios communicate better."

Start with the tension. Everything else flows downstream.

**Quick test:** Read your current headline. Does it describe you, or does it describe what the right reader is worried about? If it describes you, rewrite it.

## Step 2: Signal — Pick One Primary Archetype with One Plain-Language Promise

What should someone understand about you in one quick read?

Pick one primary archetype (from Book III). Then write one plain-language promise: what you do, for whom, and what outcome it produces.

**The one-sentence structure:** [What you do] for [whom] so they can [outcome].

Examples:
- "I build RAG pipelines and evaluation frameworks for enterprise AI teams so they can ship with confidence."
- "I design identity systems for early-career practitioners so the right opportunity finds them."
- "I teach AI engineering to builders who want to go from prototype to production."

**Quick test:** Show your headline and sub-headline to someone who doesn't already know you. Ask: "What do you think I do?" If they can't answer accurately in 10 seconds, the signal needs work.

## Step 3: Style — Make the Signal Visible in the First Second

What should the page feel like in the first second?

Use layout, typography, imagery, and contrast to make the signal visible before a word is read. Refer to your archetype's design primitives (Book II, Chapter 13 or Book IV, Chapter 2) to make consistent decisions.

**The first-second test:** Show your hero section to someone for 5 seconds, then cover it. Ask: "What did you notice first? What does this person do?" Their answer tells you what your design is actually communicating.

**The three most common style failures:**
1. Generic photography that does not reflect the archetype
2. Multiple type sizes with no clear hierarchy
3. Color pulled from trends rather than from the archetype's visual register

## Step 4: Proof — Put the Receipt Near the Promise

What artifact proves the claim fastest?

The proof should be near the promise — not three scrolls away. One specific, real, verifiable artifact is worth more than ten vague testimonials.

The proof spectrum (stronger at the top):
1. Shipped production system with documented outcomes
2. Case study with specific quantified results
3. Public code with stars or forks
4. Published article with documented reach or engagement
5. Testimonial from a named person with a real title
6. Detailed write-up showing your process
7. Demo video showing the system working

**Quick test:** Cover your copy. Is the best proof visible? Move it up if not.

## Step 5: CTA — Make the Next Step Clear, Small, and Real

What should the reader do next?

Only one CTA per page section. Make it specific, not generic. "Let's talk" is less effective than "Book a 30-minute architecture review." Make the next step feel small and safe.

**The commitment ladder:** each action should be smaller and easier than the one before it. The first CTA should feel like almost no commitment.

## Step 6: Publish — Know Where This Appears Outside the Browser

Where will this page show up outside the browser?

A page that stays only in a browser tab is not deployed. Before you launch, answer:
- Which three LinkedIn posts will summarize what this page is for?
- Which one talk or presentation could feature this work?
- Which three people will I tell directly that this is live?
- What is the QR code that goes on my card / slides / bio?

If you cannot answer these, the deployment plan is not ready.

## Step 7: Repeat — Use the Response to Shape the Next Version

What did people understand first? What did they miss?

The feedback loop (Book VI, Chapter 5) tells you what to keep, strengthen, reorder, or cut.

**The five feedback channels:**
1. What people say when you share it in conversation
2. What they click (analytics)
3. What they scroll past without stopping
4. What questions they ask after seeing it
5. What they remember when they reference you later

None of these require formal user testing. All of them require paying attention.

## The One-Screen Checklist

Before you ship, answer all five:

1. Can a stranger name the signal after a quick scan?
2. Is the best proof visible early enough?
3. Does the page feel like one person, not three mixed together?
4. Is the next step obvious?
5. Do I know where I will share this in public?

If any answer is no, fix it before launching.

## Common Mistakes

**1. Trying to sound impressive before sounding clear.** Clarity earns trust. Impressiveness after clarity earns more trust. Impressiveness without clarity earns nothing.

**2. Mixing multiple archetypes on the first screen.** The Sage headline with the Hero imagery and the Explorer color palette reads as no archetype. Pick one.

**3. Using design trends that do not support the signal.** Glassmorphism on a Sage page. Brutalist typography for a Caregiver. Visual decisions that follow your archetype are always more effective than visual decisions that follow current design trends.

**4. Hiding the proof too deep.** Seven scroll-lengths of backstory before the case study is too far. Move the proof up.

**5. Treating publishing as a bonus round.** Publishing is not optional, not extra credit, and not something you do after the page is "perfect." The page becomes more legible through public contact. Publish a version that is good enough, then improve it based on response.

## The First Build Session

If you only have one focused session to start:

1. Write one sentence about who the page is for.
2. Write one sentence about what you want them to understand about you.
3. Pick one image, artifact, or screenshot that proves it.
4. Write one CTA.
5. Show the page to one person and ask what they remember.

That is enough to start. The rest of the curriculum teaches you to do each of those five things better.

## Final Reminder

You do not need a perfect portfolio.

You need a page that gives the right person a clear read, real proof, and a reason to respond.

The right person exists. They are looking. Make it easy for them to find what they need.
