# The Updated Trivium and Quadrivium: A Formation Model for the AI Era

## Why This Matters

The medieval university organized learning around two ancient formation programs: the Trivium (grammar, logic, rhetoric) and the Quadrivium (arithmetic, geometry, music, astronomy). These were not subject areas in the modern sense — they were capacities. The goal was to produce a person who could think, communicate, and quantify. Everything else followed from that formation.

The argument this degree makes is that AI changes not what subjects are worth learning, but what capacities are worth forming. The question "what kind of person are we trying to produce?" becomes the primary design question.

The answer this curriculum gives:

> A person who can read, write, reason, quantify, compute, orchestrate, and represent responsibly in public.

Seven capacities. Not seven subjects. Not seven courses. Seven forms of capability that run through every course and every artifact in the program.

## The Updated Trivium: Language, Models, and Meaning

The Trivium governs the student's relationship to language — including the language of AI systems.

### 1. Read

Students must learn to read:

- **Sources** — technical papers, documentation, policy documents — with the capacity to identify what is claimed, what evidence supports it, what assumptions underlie it
- **Prompts and outputs** — what an LLM actually produces versus what was intended, and why the gap exists
- **Specifications and constraints** — requirements documents, API contracts, governance frameworks — with sufficient precision to build against them without guessing
- **Institutions and situations** — who has authority in a given organizational context, what the real incentive structures are, what the power dynamics are around a project

This is not basic literacy. It is a professional technical practice. The ability to read with precision — to know when you understand something, and to know when you must admit you do not — is among the most valuable and least-taught capabilities in technical education.

### 2. Write

Students must learn to write:

- **Prompts** — with the specificity, structure, and verifiability that production systems require
- **Documentation** — that other engineers can use without asking questions, that future maintainers can understand, that non-technical stakeholders can learn from
- **Product narratives** — that explain what a system does, why it was built, what it was designed not to do, and what its limitations are
- **Stakeholder explanations** — at varying levels of technical depth, without condescension or unnecessary jargon
- **Public proofs of work** — case studies, deployment write-ups, post-mortems — that make invisible technical work legible to the people who need to trust it

### 3. Reason

Students must learn to reason:

- **Under uncertainty** — making defensible decisions when information is incomplete and the cost of waiting exceeds the cost of being slightly wrong
- **Across evidence and ambiguity** — distinguishing strong evidence from weak evidence, knowing when correlation and causation are being confused, resisting conclusions the data do not actually support
- **About risks and tradeoffs** — not as an abstract exercise but as a specific discipline applied to real system architectures, deployment decisions, and design choices
- **About what a system should and should not do** — the scope and ethics of system design, not after the fact as a compliance check, but as part of the original design

## The Updated Quadrivium: Measurement, Computation, Systems, and Public Form

The Quadrivium governs the student's relationship to number, structure, and representation — updated for what AI-native work actually requires.

### 1. Quantify

Statistics, measurement, evaluation, experimentation, and model judgment.

The capacity to specify what "working" means for a specific system, build the measurement infrastructure to find out if it is working, and reason about the uncertainty and confidence in that measurement. This is not academic statistics — it is the evaluation discipline that separates production-grade AI systems from demos.

### 2. Compute

Programming, automation, software construction, and technical fluency.

Not narrow mastery of a specific language or framework, but the underlying computational thinking that makes it possible to learn any language or framework as needed — and to reason about what a system is doing even when its source code is not visible.

### 3. Orchestrate

Workflows, agents, tools, retrieval, infrastructure, and multi-system coordination.

The capacity to design, build, and operate systems that consist of multiple interacting components — databases, APIs, LLMs, vector stores, agents, user interfaces — with an understanding of how they interact, where they fail, and how to make them work reliably together.

### 4. Represent

Design, interface, media, visualization, public explanation, and signal shaping.

The capacity to take something technically real and make it legible — to a non-technical stakeholder, to a user who needs to trust the system, to a reviewer who needs to evaluate it. Representation is not decoration. It is the discipline of making invisible technical work visible and understandable to the people whose decisions depend on it.

## How This Applies to the Degree

Every course in the BSEAID spine should repeatedly cycle students through all seven capacities — not isolate each capacity in a separate silo. A data structures course should also require technical writing (write). A systems design course should also require reasoning about tradeoffs under uncertainty (reason) and representing the design to a non-technical audience (represent).

The artifact that each course produces is evidence that the student exercised multiple capacities — not just the technically obvious one. That is what makes the artifact ladder (Book X, Chapter 5) a formation record, not just a grade book.
