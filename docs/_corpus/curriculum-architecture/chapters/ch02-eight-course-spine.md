# The Eight-Course Spine: What Each Course Does and What It Produces

## The Logic of the Spine

A degree is not a list of courses. It is a formation arc — a sequence that builds capability in a deliberate order, where each course's output becomes the foundation the next course builds on.

The BSEAID spine is an eight-course sequence organized around a single question: **what kind of AI-native builder are we forming?**

The answer to that question determines the sequence, the artifacts, and the progression logic. Each course has a "degree job" — a specific contribution to the formation arc — that is separate from the course's content. A course about web development (IS218) can have a degree job of "turn the page into a real interactive product" that is also about forming the engineering discipline to make things work reliably, not just look right.

## The Eight-Course Spine

| Spine Position | Course | Degree Job | Core Question | Signature Artifact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | IS117 | Teach public page construction and first web fluency | Can you make yourself legible on the web? | First portfolio page and signal surface |
| 2 | IS265 | Introduce information systems, organizational actors, and workflow context | Do you understand the systems and institutions your product must live inside? | Systems map and problem brief |
| 3 | IS218 | Turn the page into a real interactive product with user-facing application workflows | Can you build a real interactive product? | Working web application with user-facing flows |
| 4 | IS331 | Ground students in schema, data structure, retrieval, and information architecture | Can you structure and retrieve information coherently? | Data model and retrieval-aware application layer |
| 5 | IS390 | Teach discovery, requirements articulation, and system framing from stakeholder input | Can you translate ambiguity into system requirements? | Requirements package and product definition |
| 6 | IS425 | Specialize in enterprise AI applications and infrastructure | Can you build and operate enterprise AI infrastructure responsibly? | LLM or agentic system with evaluation and deployment notes |
| 7 | IS455 | Connect technical systems to business process, organizational adoption, and operational change | Can you connect technical systems to real business process change? | Workflow redesign and automation plan |
| 8 | Selected IS course | Deepen the program's chosen edge | What is your distinctive depth? | Advanced artifact aligned to product, orchestration, or governance |

## What Each Course Produces and Why It Matters

**IS117 — The First Public Surface**
The student's first web-legible artifact. Not a classroom submission — a page that lives on the internet and communicates something real about who the student is and what they can do. This establishes the portfolio-as-product principle from day one. The artifact is not a grade; it is the beginning of a public professional record.

**IS265 — The Systems Map**
The first artifact that requires the student to think beyond the page to the organizational systems the page lives inside. A systems map and problem brief that demonstrates the student can read institutions, actors, workflows, and constraints — not just technologies.

**IS218 — The Working Application**
The first full interactive product: something real users can use, with real flows, real state, and real edge cases. This is where engineering discipline is formed: the gap between "it looks right" and "it works reliably" is the central learning.

**IS331 — The Data Model**
The first artifact that requires the student to think at the layer beneath the application: how information is structured, stored, retrieved, and made coherent at scale. A retrieval-aware application layer that demonstrates the student can reason about information architecture.

**IS390 — The Requirements Package**
The first artifact that requires the student to work from ambiguity — from a stakeholder or user problem that has not yet been defined — to a structured, defensible specification. The skill of translating human need into system requirements is among the most valuable in the forward deployed engineer profile.

**IS425 — The AI System with Evaluation**
The central AI-specific artifact: an LLM or agentic system built to handle a real problem, with an evaluation framework that demonstrates the student can specify what "working" means and measure whether the system meets that specification. The evaluation component is not optional — it is the evidence that the student has moved beyond demo-building.

**IS455 — The Workflow Redesign**
The artifact that requires the student to think about organizational consequence: how does a technical system change the behavior of the people and institutions that use it? A workflow redesign and automation plan that demonstrates the student can connect technical systems to real organizational change.

**The Advanced Depth Course — The Distinctive Edge**
The final artifact is deliberately flexible, depending on the program's chosen emphasis:
- If the degree leans product and deployment: a UX research or product research artifact
- If the degree leans LLM systems and orchestration: a deeper AI infrastructure artifact
- If the degree leans governance and enterprise change: a policy, measurement, or organizational change artifact

The artifact must be externally legible — understandable to someone who did not take the course — and must complete the artifact ladder rather than break it.

## The Supporting Courses

These courses reinforce the spine without being part of the eight-course narrative sequence:

- DS110 for AI and data foundations
- CS118 for programming depth
- IS247, IS375, IS448 for UX research and usability
- IS392, IS480 for AI and data specialization
- MGMT 480, IE 492 or ENTR 210 for innovation and organizational framing
- IS491 or IT 491 for capstone execution

## The Spine as a Formation Record

At the end of the eight-course spine, the student has produced eight artifacts. Together, those artifacts are:

1. A public answer to "can you make yourself legible?" (IS117)
2. A public answer to "do you understand organizations?" (IS265)
3. A public answer to "can you build real things?" (IS218)
4. A public answer to "do you understand information?" (IS331)
5. A public answer to "can you work from ambiguity?" (IS390)
6. A public answer to "can you build AI systems responsibly?" (IS425)
7. A public answer to "can you drive organizational change?" (IS455)
8. A public answer to "what is your distinctive depth?" (Final course)

These are not eight assignments. They are eight public claims with evidence. That is what the employer evaluates. That is what the portfolio is for.
