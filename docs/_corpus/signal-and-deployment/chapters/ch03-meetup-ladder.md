# The Meetup Ladder

## Why Meetups Matter More Than LinkedIn

LinkedIn is a broadcast channel. You publish, you hope it reaches the right people, you rarely know whether it did.

Meetups are a presence channel. You show up, you are in the room, and your signal is tested in real time by real human response.

The difference matters for weak tie formation: LinkedIn creates passive exposure; meetups create active contact. Both are important. Meetups are less scalable but higher-conversion for the specific purpose of forming remembered connections in a specific professional community.

## The Ladder: Six Rungs

**Rung 1: Attend**

Go to the meetup. This seems obvious — but many people who say they want to build a network do not consistently show up. Attendance is the prerequisite for everything. No attendance, no presence, no contact, no weak ties.

Actionable: Identify two or three meetups in your field or target field. Put the next three months of events on your calendar. Show up at a minimum of one per month.

**Rung 2: Talk to People**

Not everyone at the event. A small number of real conversations is more valuable than many business card exchanges.

The approach does not require charisma or extroversion. Pick one person who is standing alone or between conversations. Ask one genuine question about their work. Listen to the answer. Tell them one honest thing about your work. See where it goes.

The goal at rung 2: form three to five real conversations per event where both parties have a clear, accurate sense of what the other person does.

**Rung 3: Meet the Organizer**

Every recurring professional meetup has an organizer. This person is the connective tissue of the community — they know everyone, they understand the audience, and they are often the first person who can describe you accurately to others who ask.

Approach the organizer directly. Tell them what you do and that you appreciate the event. Ask how they select speakers. That single question plants the seed for Rung 5.

**Rung 4: Be Remembered**

Between events, do something that keeps your signal alive for the people you met. This can be:
- A LinkedIn post that specifically references the topic someone brought up when you talked
- A short follow-up note that references something specific from the conversation
- Sharing something useful that's directly relevant to a problem someone mentioned

The goal: when the organizer or another attendee sees your name again, they remember the specific conversation and the specific thing you do.

**Rung 5: Ask for a Short Talk Slot**

Most professional meetups, particularly in technology fields, actively need speakers. The barrier to speaking is much lower than most people assume. A 10-to-15-minute talk on something you have actually done is sufficient.

The ask: go to the organizer after you have attended several times and been remembered. Say: "I have been thinking about giving a short talk on [specific topic that is relevant to this audience]. Is that something you would consider for a future event?"

The topic specificity matters. "A talk on AI engineering" will get a vague response. "A 12-minute talk on how I built an evaluation harness that caught 12 prompt regressions in the first 60 days" is a specific, credible proposal.

**Rung 6: Present and Build Authority**

After you speak, something shifts. You have become a person who has presented in this community. Your name now attaches to a specific idea for everyone in that room. The weak ties you formed passively through attendance now have a stronger signal to carry.

After the talk:
- Post a brief summary on LinkedIn with a link to your portfolio or the key artifact
- Follow up with two or three people who specifically asked questions or came to talk afterward
- Start thinking about the next talk slot — continuing the cycle

## The Ladder is Slow

Moving from Rung 1 to Rung 6 takes months, not weeks. This is not a failure of the system — it is how trust and presence accumulate in real human networks.

The people who get frustrated with this ladder are usually trying to skip rungs. They want to speak without having attended consistently, or get referrals without having had real conversations. The ladder cannot be rushed without losing the trust-building that makes each rung valuable.

Consistent, patient, specific engagement — that is the mechanism. The opportunity shows up as a consequence.
