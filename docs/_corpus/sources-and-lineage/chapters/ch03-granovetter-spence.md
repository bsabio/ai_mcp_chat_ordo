# Granovetter, Spence, and the Economics of Signal

## Mark Granovetter: The Strength of Weak Ties

### Who He Is

Mark Granovetter is an American sociologist and professor at Stanford. His foundational work in economic sociology transformed how researchers think about the relationship between social network structure and economic outcomes.

### The Central Work

**"The Strength of Weak Ties"** (1973, *American Journal of Sociology*) — one of the most cited papers in social science. Extended and applied in **"The Strength of Weak Ties: A Network Theory Revisited"** (1983).

### What He Found

The commonsense assumption about networks is that strong ties — close friends, family, people you interact with frequently — are your most valuable sources of support and opportunity. Granovetter's research reversed this expectation.

**Weak ties (acquaintances) provide access to information and opportunities that strong ties do not.** The mechanism: strong ties share your social world. They know the same people, operate in the same spaces, and have access to the same information. Weak ties provide bridging — connection to social worlds you do not otherwise have access to. New opportunities, including job opportunities, flow through these bridges precisely because they connect otherwise disconnected networks.

**Job seekers find employment more often through weak ties than through close friends or formal applications.** The original research on this was conducted through interviews with professional, technical, and managerial workers who had recently changed jobs.

**Implication for public signal:** If weak ties are the primary mechanism for new opportunity, then the relevant design question for a portfolio or career positioning system is: how accurately and enthusiastically can a weak tie describe you to someone you have never met? The signal clarity is the infrastructure for opportunity.

### Where This Appears in the Curriculum

**Book VI, Chapter 2 (Weak Ties and the Opportunity Network)** — Granovetter's research is the primary evidence base for why public signal coherence and repeated presence in professional spaces produces compounding opportunity. The meetup ladder (Book VI, Chapter 3) is built on this mechanism.

## Michael Spence: Signaling Theory

### Who He Is

Michael Spence is an American economist, Stanford professor, and Nobel laureate in economics (2001). His Nobel Prize was awarded for his foundational work on markets with asymmetric information, specifically his theory of market signaling.

### The Central Work

**"Job Market Signaling"** (1973, *Quarterly Journal of Economics*) — the paper that introduced the formal model of signaling under information asymmetry.

### What He Found

In job markets, employers cannot directly observe the capabilities of applicants. Applicants know more about their own abilities than employers do. This information asymmetry creates a market failure: high-capability and low-capability applicants look identical to employers before hiring.

**Signaling is the mechanism by which capable workers establish credibility.** A signal is an observable characteristic that is costly to acquire for low-capability individuals but less costly for high-capability ones. If this condition holds, signals can carry genuine information — the very fact that someone has acquired the signal is evidence of the underlying capability.

**The portfolio-as-signal implication:** The investment required to produce a specific, well-evidenced, publicly deployed portfolio artifact is itself a signal of the capability and discipline it takes to build it. The effort and specificity of the signal are not just communication — they are also evidence.

**Costly signals are more credible than cheap ones.** A vague self-description costs almost nothing to produce and thus carries almost no information about capability. A real case study documenting a specific system built and deployed with verifiable outcomes is costly to produce and thus carries real signal.

### Where This Appears in the Curriculum

**Book VI, Chapter 2 (Weak Ties and the Opportunity Network)** — Spence provides the economic foundation for why specific, high-effort artifacts are more credible than generic, low-effort claims.

**Book V, Chapter 1 (Trust Is Not a Mood)** — the structural definition of trustworthy evidence draws on the signaling logic: proof that is costly to fake is proof that carries information.

## Combined Implication

Granovetter and Spence together explain the deployment mechanism system:

Granovetter explains where opportunity comes from: through weak ties who carry your signal to social worlds you cannot reach directly.

Spence explains why specific, high-effort signals are credible: because their production cost is itself evidence of the underlying capability they claim.

Together: build a specific, costly signal — not because it looks impressive up close, but because it can be accurately carried by people who know you lightly to people you have never met.
