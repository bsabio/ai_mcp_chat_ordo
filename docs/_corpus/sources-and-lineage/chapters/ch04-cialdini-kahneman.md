# Cialdini and Kahneman: Influence, Judgment, and Bounded Rationality

## Robert Cialdini: The Six Principles of Influence

### Who He Is

Robert Cialdini is a professor of psychology and marketing at Arizona State University and the author of *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984). His research on social influence is among the most replicated in applied psychology.

### The Central Work

**"Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion"** (1984) — the primary synthesis of his research on compliance and social influence. The academic version of much of this research appeared in the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* and other peer-reviewed venues.

**"Pre-Suasion: A Revolutionary Way to Influence and Persuade"** (2016) — subsequent work on the conditions that precede persuasion.

### What He Found

Six principles reliably shape compliance and trust decisions:

1. **Reciprocity**: People feel obligated to give back when they receive something first.
2. **Commitment/Consistency**: After making a small commitment, people are more likely to make related larger commitments.
3. **Social proof**: People look to similar others when making uncertain decisions.
4. **Authority**: People defer to experts and established authorities.
5. **Liking**: People are more easily influenced by those they like.
6. **Scarcity**: Things become more attractive when they are less available.

**The critical insight for this curriculum:** These principles work most powerfully when they are embedded in the structure of an interaction — not when they are applied as visible persuasion tactics. A page that gives something genuinely useful (reciprocity, embedded) is different from a page with a "FREE REPORT" popup (reciprocity, visible and cheap).

### Where This Appears in the Curriculum

**Book V (Trust, Proof and Persuasion)** — the full treatment: Chapter 1 (the structural definition of trust), Chapter 2 (the Cialdini principles as embedded architecture).

## Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky: Behavioral Economics and Bounded Rationality

### Who They Are

Daniel Kahneman is a psychologist and Nobel laureate in economics (2002) and professor emeritus at Princeton. Amos Tversky (1937–1996) was his longtime collaborator at Hebrew University of Jerusalem and Stanford.

### The Central Work

**"Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases"** (1974, *Science*) — the paper that launched behavioral economics.

**"Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision Under Risk"** (1979, *Econometrica*) — the foundational model of how people actually make risky decisions, dramatically different from classical rational actor models.

**"Thinking, Fast and Slow"** by Kahneman (2011) — the accessible synthesis of their research for general audiences.

### What They Found

People do not make decisions by exhaustively evaluating all relevant information and selecting the utility-maximizing option. They use **heuristics** — efficient cognitive shortcuts that are generally accurate but systematically err in predictable ways.

The System 1 / System 2 distinction (popularized in Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*):
- **System 1**: fast, automatic, intuitive, low-effort. Governs first impressions, immediate judgments, pattern recognition.
- **System 2**: slow, deliberate, analytical, high-effort. Governs careful evaluation, mathematical reasoning, explicit comparison.

**Heuristics with direct curriculum implications:**

- **The halo effect**: A positive impression in one dimension (visual credibility) carries over to others (capability evaluation) — which is why visual design decisions are not separable from trust decisions.
- **Anchoring**: The first number or reference point encountered disproportionately shapes subsequent evaluation — which is why leading with your strongest work matters.
- **Fluency heuristic**: Things that are easier to process feel more true and more credible — which is the cognitive science basis for the trust advantages of grid discipline and typographic clarity.
- **Loss aversion**: Losses feel approximately twice as intense as equivalent gains — which is why loss framing (what happens if you do not solve this problem) motivates more strongly than gain framing when both are accurate.
